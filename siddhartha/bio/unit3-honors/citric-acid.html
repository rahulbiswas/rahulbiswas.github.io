The Citric Acid Cycle

Essential Understanding

The citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle) is a central metabolic hub that completely oxidizes acetyl-CoA to CO2, generating energy carriers NADH and FADH2 for ATP production.

Overview

Basic Information

  • Occurs in mitochondrial matrix
  • Cyclical process - regenerates start molecule
  • Connects multiple metabolic pathways
  • Major energy-harvesting process

Products Per Turn

  • 2 CO2 molecules released
  • 6 NADH molecules produced
  • 2 FADH2 molecules produced
  • 2 ATP generated directly

Key Steps

Citrate Formation

  • Acetyl-CoA combines with oxaloacetate
  • Forms 6-carbon citrate
  • Releases CoA
  • Irreversible step

Oxidative Steps

  • Two carbons removed as CO2
  • Electrons transferred to NAD+ and FAD
  • Multiple enzyme complexes
  • Energy harvesting steps

Cycle Completion

  • Regenerates oxaloacetate
  • Ready for next acetyl-CoA
  • Continuous operation possible
  • Links to electron transport chain

Regulatory Factors

Clinical Connections

Disorders & Diseases

  • Mitochondrial diseases
  • Metabolic disorders

  • Cancer metabolism

Quick Check

Why is the citric acid cycle called an amphibolic pathway?

It's amphibolic because it functions in both catabolism (breaking down molecules) and anabolism (building molecules), serving as a central hub where many metabolic pathways intersect.
