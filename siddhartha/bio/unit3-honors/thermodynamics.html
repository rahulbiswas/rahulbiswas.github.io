Laws of Thermodynamics

Essential Understanding

Two fundamental laws govern energy transformations in biological systems: energy cannot be created or destroyed, and entropy tends to increase in all processes.

First Law of Thermodynamics

Key Principles

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed
  • Energy can be converted between forms
  • Total energy remains constant
  • Energy must be obtained from environment

Biological Applications

  • Energy input required for work
  • Food as energy source
  • Energy transformations in metabolism
  • Heat production in reactions

Second Law of Thermodynamics

Core Concepts

  • Entropy increases in spontaneous processes
  • Energy transfers produce heat
  • Perfect efficiency impossible
  • Disorder tends to increase

Cellular Implications

  • Maintaining order requires energy
  • Heat lost in all processes
  • Limited metabolic efficiency
  • Need for constant energy input

Quick Check

Why do cells need constant energy input to maintain organization?

According to the second law, systems naturally tend toward disorder (increasing entropy). Cells must constantly use energy to maintain their highly ordered state against this natural tendency.
