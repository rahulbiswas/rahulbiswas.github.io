Hydrogen Bonding

Essential Understanding

Hydrogen bonds are weak intermolecular forces that form between a hydrogen atom bonded to a highly electronegative atom and another electronegative atom.

Characteristics of Hydrogen Bonds

Requirements for Hydrogen Bonding

  • Hydrogen bonded to N, O, or F
  • Electronegative atom with lone pair
  • Proper molecular orientation

Types and Strength

Bond Strength

  • Weaker than covalent bonds
  • Stronger than van der Waals forces
  • Energy: 4-30 kJ/mol

Biological Importance

DNA Structure

  • Base pairing in DNA double helix
  • A-T: 2 hydrogen bonds
  • G-C: 3 hydrogen bonds

Protein Structure

  • Stabilizes secondary structures
  • Alpha helices
  • Beta pleated sheets

Cell Membranes

  • Phospholipid interactions
  • Membrane protein stability
  • Transport across membranes

Properties Due to H-Bonds

  • High boiling point
  • High heat of vaporization
  • Less dense solid form (ice)
  • Surface tension

Quick Check

Why do G-C base pairs create a stronger bond than A-T pairs?

G-C pairs form three hydrogen bonds while A-T pairs only form two, making G-C pairs more stable.
