Enzyme Structure

Essential Understanding

Enzymes are proteins with specific three-dimensional structures that determine their catalytic function and specificity.

Basic Structure

Key Components

  • Protein backbone
  • Active site pocket
  • Substrate binding region
  • Catalytic residues
  • Allosteric sites (if present)

Active Site

Characteristics

  • Specific 3D shape
  • Complementary to substrate
  • Contains catalytic amino acids
  • Often deep pocket or cleft

Lock-and-Key vs. Induced Fit

  • Lock-and-Key: rigid structure
  • Induced Fit: flexible conformations
  • Modern view favors induced fit

Cofactors and Coenzymes

Cofactors

  • Metal ions (Zn2+, Mg2+, Fe2+)
  • Directly bound to enzyme
  • Structural or catalytic roles

Coenzymes

  • Organic molecules
  • Often vitamin derivatives
  • Examples: NAD+, FAD, coenzyme A
  • Transfer electrons or groups

Structural Features

Domains

  • Catalytic domain
  • Regulatory domain
  • Binding domain

Specificity Determinants

  • Shape complementarity
  • Chemical interactions
  • Amino acid positioning

Structure-Function Relationship

  • Shape determines substrate binding
  • Amino acid properties affect catalysis
  • Flexibility affects activity
  • Mutations can alter function

Quick Check

Why is the induced fit model more accurate than lock-and-key?

The induced fit model accounts for protein flexibility and shows how enzymes change shape to better fit their substrates, leading to optimal catalysis.
